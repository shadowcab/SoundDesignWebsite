Introduction to Guitar Effects

In The Beginning...

Amplified guitars started appearing on the music scene back in the 1930’s to compete with the loud horn players of the big swing bands. But the organic sound of those early amplified acoustic guitars was thin and not very pleasing. Then in the mid to late 1930’s, Harold DeArmond invented the first commercial guitar pickup which paved the way for a variety of portable, standalone effects so players could create their unique sounds. As the story goes, DeArmond used a fret tapping technique to demonstrate the sensitivity of his pickup designs.

The first commercially available standalone effects box was a tremolo unit invented by DeArmond and mass produced in 1948. By the late 1950’s, tremolo, vibrato, echo, and reverb guitar effects started appearing on the scene. Most effects at that time were made with vacuum tubes and built directly into the amplifier. By the early 1960's, more portable, standalone effects units became available as transistors replaced bulky and fragile vacuum tubes. Guitarists were no longer limited to the fixed configuration of their amplifiers built-in effects. You can read more about the history of guitar effects pedals here (Davies, 2018).

Motivation for Tone

Today, thousands of sound-altering devices are available for electric guitars in the form of effects pedals (aka stompbox) and multi-effects. Electric guitarists quickly discovered that guitar effects could be connected and arranged in an infinite number of ways to completely dress-up or down their guitar tone as appropriate for the music. Fat and silky or throaty and warm tones could be reproduced by modifying the amplitude and frequency content of their guitar signal. The shape of the audio wave form could also be transformed or translated into something very different and fresh.

Should you decide to hit the eject button now and skip all the upcoming theory, be sure to checkout this website (In The Studio: Recreate The Setups Of Classic Guitar Gods, 2013) and discover how some of the greatest classic rock guitarists achieved their tone.

Types of Effects

Although, there are thousands of effects pedals on the market, they essentially fall into one of three effects categories: dynamic (like boosts, compressors, limiters, and noise gates), spectral (like EQ’s and enhancers), or time-based (which include echo, delay, chorus, flanger, phase shifting, reverb, pitch transposers, and harmonizers). I dedicate a whole section on the different types of effects here. After a brief overview, you can then dive deeper into the separate types: dynamic, spectral, or time-based.

Signal Chain

An effect can be incorporated as a "stand alone" unit where your guitar connects to the input jack of the effect and an amplifier connected to the ouput jack. But the real fun begins when you connect multipe effects pedals into your signal path or signal chain. There are some general guidelines by which you can establish the intitial "order" of your effects. I've dedicated an entire page to discuss signal chain and how you can vary the order of effects to achieve different tonal affects.

The Line 6 Helix is a professional grade multi-effects platform for guitarist and bassists. The display on the Helix depicts each effect as a box around a symbol. Altogether, these symbols or blocks and their connections represent the signal chain that the guitar signal will pass through. Each block represents a sound-altering device such as an effects pedal, amplifier, or speaker cabinet. An infinite number of guitar tones are possible based on what effects you choose and the order that you connect them together. See Fig. 1 - The Line 6 Helix Signal Chain.

It's not unusual to connect two identical pedals in the signal chain and then dialing in different parameters on each. This is called "stacking" your effects and it adds a whole new dimension to your guitar tone. For example, some guitarists will place two distortion pedals next to each other in the signal chain. The first pedal is used to create a light overdrive effect at the point where the signal starts to break-up and the second identical pedal will be configured to produce non-transparent distortion with more sustain. Similarly, guitarists will use multiple delay effects to create two simultaneous but distintly different echo patterns which play off of each other.

Form-Factor

Effects come in various formats including single-effects stompboxes (Fig. 2), multi-effects (Fig. 3 & Fig. 4), rack-mounted, and software patches and add-ons that run in a Digital Audio Workstation (DAW).

If you like experimenting with different effects and sounds, a multi-effects unit may cost less in the long run since they are usually packed with more effects than you can fit on a single pedalboard. Some also emulate several legendary amp and cabinet models for you to tryout.
